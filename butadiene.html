Particle in a Box: Butadiene Example

The particle-in-a-box model can be applied to the π electrons in linear conjugated hydrocarbons such as butadiene (H2C=CH–CH=CH2), which contains four π electrons. Although butadiene is not linear in reality, for simplicity, we assume the π electrons move along a straight line.

Model and Parameters

The length of the "box" is estimated as the total distance covered by the π electrons:

  • 2 C=C bond lengths: \( 2 \times 135 \, \text{pm} = 270 \, \text{pm} \)
  • 1 C–C bond length: \( 154 \, \text{pm} \)
  • Two carbon atom radii: \( 2 \times 77 \, \text{pm} = 154 \, \text{pm} \)

Total length: \( 578 \, \text{pm} \) or \( 578 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{m} \).

Energy Levels

The allowed energy levels are given by:

\( E_n = \frac{h^2 n^2}{8m a^2}, \, n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \)

Here, \( h \) is Planck's constant, \( m \) is the mass of an electron (\( 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg} \)), and \( a \) is the box length (\( 578 \, \text{pm} \)).

Transition Energy

By the Pauli Exclusion Principle, each energy state can hold two electrons with opposite spins. For butadiene, the four π electrons occupy the \( n = 1 \) and \( n = 2 \) levels. The energy difference for a transition from \( n = 2 \) to \( n = 3 \) is:

\( \Delta E = \frac{h^2}{8m a^2} \left(3^2 - 2^2\right) \)

Substituting values:

\( \Delta E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})^2 \cdot (9)}{8 \cdot (9.109 \times 10^{-31}) \cdot (578 \times 10^{-12})^2} = 9.02 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \)

The corresponding wavenumber is:

\( \tilde{\nu} = \frac{\Delta E}{hc} = \frac{9.02 \times 10^{-19}}{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)} = 4.54 \times 10^4 \, \text{cm}^{-1} \)

Comparison to Experimental Data

Butadiene has an experimental absorption band at \( 4.61 \times 10^4 \, \text{cm}^{-1} \). This shows that the particle-in-a-box model provides a reasonable approximation for explaining the absorption spectrum of conjugated systems like butadiene.
