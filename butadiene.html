Particle in a Box: Butadiene Example

The particle-in-a-box model can be applied to model the π electrons in linear conjugated hydrocarbons such as butadiene (H2C=CH–CH=CH2), which contains four π electrons. Although butadiene is not linear in reality, for simplicity, we assume the π electrons move along a straight line.

Model and Parameters

The length of the "box" is estimated as the total distance covered by the π electrons. We calculate that distance by using the single and double bond experimental distances, and then add a "buffer" on both sides of half a single bond, to model the fact that the space available to the electrons doesn't stop over the nucleus but extends a bit further:

  • 2 C=C bond lengths: \( 2 \times 135 \, \text{pm} = 270 \, \text{pm} \)
  • 1 C–C bond length: \( 154 \, \text{pm} \)
  • Two carbon atom radii: \( 2 \times 77 \, \text{pm} = 154 \, \text{pm} \)

Total length a: \( 578 \, \text{pm} \) or \( 578 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{m} \).

Energy Levels

The allowed energy levels for a particle in a box are given by:

\( E_n = \frac{h^2 n^2}{8m a^2}, \, n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \)

Where:

  • \( h \): Planck's constant (\( 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s} \))
  • \( m \): Electron mass (\( 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg} \))
  • \( a \): Box length (\( 578 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{m} \))

Electron Distribution and Pauli Exclusion Principle

By the Pauli Exclusion Principle, each energy level can hold a maximum of two electrons with opposite spins. In the case of butadiene, which has four π electrons, the first two energy levels (\( n = 1 \) and \( n = 2 \)) are fully occupied, while higher levels (\( n = 3 \) and \( n = 4 \)) remain empty. The highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) is \( n = 2 \), and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) is \( n = 3 \).

Transition Energy

The energy difference for a transition from \( n = 2 \) (HOMO) to \( n = 3 \) (LUMO) is:

\[ \Delta E = \frac{h^2}{8m a^2} \cdot \left( 3^2 - 2^2 \right) = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s})^2 \cdot 5}{8 \cdot (9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}) \cdot (578 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{m})^2} \]

Substituting values, \( \Delta E = 9.02 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \).

Wavenumber

The corresponding wavenumber \( \tilde{\nu} \), in \( \text{cm}^{-1} \), is calculated as:

\[ \tilde{\nu} = \frac{\Delta E}{h \cdot c} = \frac{9.02 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}}{(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s}) \cdot (2.998 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s})} \]

Converting to \( \text{cm}^{-1} \), \( \tilde{\nu} = 4.54 \times 10^4 \, \text{cm}^{-1} \).

Comparison to Experimental Data

Butadiene has an experimental absorption band at \( 4.61 \times 10^4 \, \text{cm}^{-1} \). This shows that the particle-in-a-box model provides a reasonable approximation for explaining the absorption spectrum of conjugated systems like butadiene. Unfortunately the same accuracy doesn't hold for longer conjugated systems.
